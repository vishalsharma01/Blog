    Frequentists vs Bayesians

    It is the never ending debate in the world of statistics.

    Do you follow Frequentism or are you a believer in Bayesianism?

    In this post we are going to explore both approaches, establish the main differences and analyze how they are used to solve statistical problems.

    Let's start off by defining the two:

    Frequentists:

    For followers of frequentism, the belief is that 'probability' only has meaning in terms of a limiting case of repeated measurements.

    Frequentists attempt to calculate the probability of an event occuring by conducting the same experiment over and over again, under the same exact conditions, to predict what the possible outcome would be.

    Basically, frequentist statistics attempts to test whether an event will occur or not.

    Let's take a look at a quick example:

    I am going to use Pandas to spin up a table that will help us get a better understanding of this.

    import pandas as pd

    example = pd.DataFrame({
    'no. of tosses': [10, 50, 100, 500, 1000, 5000, 10000],
    'no. of heads': [4, 25, 44, 255, 502, 2533, 5067],
    'difference': [-1, 0, -6, 5, 2, 33, 67]
})
example
    no. of tosses no. of heads difference
    0 10 4 -1
    1 50 25 0
    2 100 44 -6
    3 500 255 5
    4 1000 502 2
    5 5000 2533 33
    6 10000 5067 67

    Using this table we are trying to determine the fairness of a given coin. Because a coin flip can only have two possible outcomes (heads or tails) we can establish the fact that each has a probability of 0.5 (50%).

    What the table is trying to depict is: the total # of coin tosses, how many heads we get and the difference.

    In the first row we have 10 tosses of which we got 4 heads which means we have a difference of 1. What this means is that since we are using a "fair" coin where the probability of a head or tail is equally 50% we only managed to get 4 heads giving us an unequal difference of 1 againt the total number of tails we got which would be 6.
